1.1 Introduction

Metal forming can be described as a process in which the desired size and shape are obtained through the deformation of metals plastically under the action of externally applied forces. These processes exploit the property of plasticityâ€”the ability to flow as solids without deterioration of properties.

Key Advantage: Since the material is rearranged rather than cut away, waste is substantially reduced compared to machining.

Process Categories

  • Direct-Compression: E.g., Forging and Rolling.
  • Indirect-Compression: E.g., Wire drawing and Extrusion.
  • Tension Type: E.g., Stretch forming.
  • Bending & Shearing: Application of bending moments or shearing forces.

1.1.1 Independent & Dependent Variables

Forming processes are complex systems. To optimize them, we must understand the variables we control versus those determined by the process.

Independent Variables (Direct Control)

  • Starting material & Geometry: Selection of billets, slabs, or blooms.
  • Tool/Die Geometry: Diameter of rolls or die angles.
  • Lubrication: Acts as coolant and reduces friction (often >50% of power used).
  • Starting Temperature & Speed: Critical for material behavior and production rates.

Dependent Variables (System Determined)

  • Force/Power Requirements: Predicted to specify equipment.
  • Final Material Properties: Altered by deformation and heat.
  • Exit Temperature & Surface Finish: Impacted by internal heat generation and tool contact.

1.2 Hot Working

Plastic deformation carried out above the recrystallization temperature (typically 0.3Tm to 0.6Tm). New, strain-free grains form, replacing the distorted structures.

Advantages:
  • Lower power requirements for large deformations.
  • Refines grain structure and minimizes porosity.
  • No work-hardening occurs during the process.
Disadvantages:
  • Rapid oxidation and scaling of the surface.
  • Poor dimensional tolerances due to thermal contraction.
  • High tooling and maintenance costs.

1.4 Comparison of Hot and Cold Working

Feature Hot Working Cold Working
Temperature Above Recrystallization Below Recrystallization
Strength/Hardness Minimal change Increased (Strain Hardening)
Surface Finish Poor (Oxidation/Scaling) Good/Bright finish
Force Required Lower Higher
Ductility Maintained Reduced

1.5 Warm Working

Conducted at intermediate temperatures, offering a compromise between hot and cold forming.

  • Vs. Cold Working: Lower loads and greater ductility.
  • Vs. Hot Working: Better precision, less scaling, and longer tool life.
