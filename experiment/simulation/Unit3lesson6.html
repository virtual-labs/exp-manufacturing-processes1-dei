6.1 Introduction

Products made by sheet-metal forming processes are all around us. They include utensils, file cabinets, appliances, vehicle bodies etc. Compared to casting and forging, sheet-metal parts offer the advantages of lightweight and versatile shapes. Because of low cost and generally good strength and formability characteristics, low-carbon steel is the most commonly used sheet metal.

Material Economy: Sheet metal reduces material requirements and manufacturing time to less than half compared to machining. The entire top of a car can be finished to size from a single metal sheet.

6.2 Punches and Dies

Sheet metal operations are usually carried by using a punch and die. The die has the negative shape of the contour, while the punch has the positive contour of the shape to be produced.

Figure 6.2: Operation of punch and die showing clearance for sheet thickness

6.3 Sheet Metal Working Operations

6.3.1 Piercing & Blanking

Piercing: Producing a hole where the punched-out material is waste.
Blanking: Cutting a piece where the punched-out metal is the required product (the "blank").

6.3.3 Notching

Removal of metal of the desired shape from the edge of the plate.

6.3.4 Beading & Hemming

Beading: Rolling the edge for strength and safety.
Hemming: Folding the edge over and pressing it flat to create a non-jagged edge.

6.3.7 Seaming

Joining two sheets at the edges without fasteners (e.g., beverage cans).

6.4 Mechanism of Blanking

The process involves three distinct stages of shear:

  1. Elastic Deformation: The punch presses the sheet initially.
  2. Plastic Deformation: The punch penetrates, and tensile stresses exceed the material's ultimate strength.
  3. Fracture: Cracks propagate from the punch and die edges, leading to a tensile cleavage fracture.

6.5 Drawing & Wire Drawing

6.5.1 Drawing

Shaping a flat blank into a 3D hollow component. If the depth-to-diameter ratio is > 0.4, it is Deep Drawing. Blank holders are used to prevent wrinkling caused by compressive stresses.

6.8 Wire Drawing

Pulling metal through a die using tensile force. This reduces the material to very small diameters with excellent surface finish. Ductility is essential; materials are often annealed first.

6.6 Coining & Embossing

Coining: Cold squeezing where all surfaces are confined. Used for coins and medals. High pressure (5-6x material strength) is used. Lubricants are not used as they prevent fine detail reproduction.

Embossing: Producing raised or depressed designs. It is a stretching operation rather than a squeezing one and requires lower pressure than coining.
